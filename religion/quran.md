> **Al-Kahf** 80: "'And as for the boy [Al-Khidr killed], his parents were true believers, and we feared that he would pressure them into defiance and disbelief. / So we hoped that their Lord would give them another, more virtuous and caring in his place."

While the idea of God knowing the outcome of human free choices and acting upon them seems similar to Christian (and particularly Thomist) perspectives on predestination, this story of the killing of an innocent child due to his future bad choices before he has even made those choices seems troubling to me. As St Paul emphasises, God elected Jacob over Esau while they were still in their mother's womb and before Esau had done anything bad, but the consequences of that election only played out over time and in response to Esau's actual choices (as foreknown by God).

> **At-Takwir** 29: "But you cannot will to do so unless Allah — Lord of all worlds — wills."

Classical theologians use this verse to illustrate the distinction between Allah's universal/ontological will (*al-mashī'ah al-kawniyyah*) and His legislative will (*al-mashī'ah al-shar'iyyah*). The universal will encompasses everything that comes to pass; nothing happens in creation except by Allah's permission, including acts of disobedience. The legislative will refers to what Allah commands and approves of: righteousness, worship, justice. So a sinful act occurs by God's universal will (He permits it to happen) but against His legislative will (He forbids and disapproves of it). This distinction is central to how Islamic theology reconciles divine sovereignty with human moral responsibility.
